1. The Accusative is Easy

Feminine (die), Neuter (das), and Plural (die) stay exactly identical to Nominative. Only Masculine (der) changes! It drops its endings and puts on the -en "hat" (den / einen / keinen).

2. The Dative "M-R-M-N" Rule

In the Dative, look at the endings of the definite articles (dem, der, dem, den). They follow the sequence: M - R - M - N. Masculine and Neuter share identical Dative words (dem, einem).

3. The Dative Plural "Double N"

In Dative plural, the article always becomes den / keinen / unseren, and the noun itself steals an extra -n at the end if it doesn't already have one (e.g., mit den Kindern).

2. Accusative-Only Prepositions

These prepositions always command the Accusative case, no matter what:

für (for)  |  ohne (without)  |  durch (through)  |  gegen (against)

🍀 Dative Triggers

1. Dative Verbs (Giving & Helping)

2. Dative-Only Prepositions

These "Dative bullies" always command the Dative case, no matter what:

mit (with)  |  zu (to)  |  bei (at/with)  |  nach (after/to)
aus (from/out of)  |  von (from)  |  gegenüber (opposite)

🔄 Two-Way Prepositions (Wechselpräpositionen)

Prepositions like in, auf, an, unter, über, neben, hinter, vor, zwischen change case depending on the geological result of the movement.

Direction / Destination (Accusative)

Wohin? (Where to?)

Movement crossing a border, going from point A into point B.

Ich gehe in den Park. (der Park -> den)

Static Location / Inside (Dative)

Wo? (Where?)

No boundary is crossed. You are moving around inside a fixed boundary.

Ich jogge im (in dem) Park. (der Park -> dem)
